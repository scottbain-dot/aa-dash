Myth busters

Done with the form? Flip the cards below — each one is something you've probably heard. Tap to see whether it holds up.

Myth 01
"Energy drinks give you long-lasting energy."
Mostly false Energy drinks give you a short caffeine + sugar spike followed by a crash. They're not "fuel" — they're stimulants with sugar. For sustained energy in sport, food and a sensible pre-game meal beats them every time.
Myth 02
"You need a protein shake right after training or you'll lose your gains."
Exaggerated The "anabolic window" is wider than 30 minutes. Eating decent protein within a few hours of training is what matters. A sandwich, milk, or a normal meal works fine — shakes are convenient, not magic.
Myth 03
"Carbs are bad and make you slow."
False For most sports, carbs are the #1 fuel for working muscles. Skipping them tanks your performance. The type matters (whole grains, fruit, veg over refined sugar most of the time) but cutting carbs as an athlete is a bad call.
Myth 04
"Skipping breakfast is fine, you'll burn more fat."
Bad for performance For an athlete training in school PE or before a game, skipping breakfast means you start the day under-fuelled. You'll feel flat, lose focus, and underperform. Fat loss isn't the goal — performance is.
Myth 05
"Eating fat makes you fat."
False Healthy fats (avocado, nuts, olive oil, oily fish) are essential for hormones, brain function, and joints. What makes you gain unwanted weight is a sustained surplus of total energy — not fat specifically.
Myth 06
"Athletes need supplements to perform."
Mostly false For 99% of school-age athletes, real food covers it. Supplements are a tiny edge at the very top end. Get your sleep, hydration, and three good meals dialled before even thinking about a tub of anything.
Myth 07
"Sports drinks are necessary for any workout."
Mostly false For a normal PE session or short game, water is fine. Sports drinks earn their place in long efforts (over 60–90 min) or hot conditions where you're losing meaningful sweat and fuel.
Myth 08
"You should eat right before competing for energy."
Depends A big meal too close to competition can leave you sluggish or queasy. A proper meal 2–3 hours before, and a small top-up snack 30–60 min before, is the usual play.

Fuel for the working athlete.

Your body uses three energy systems. Most sports rely on a mix, and that mix tells you what to eat and when. Then we'll layer on timing — before, during, and the basics of hydration.

The three energy systems

Red
Up to ~10 seconds

Maximum effort, instant power. Used for jumps, sprints, lifts, the first explosive move.

Fuel: ATP + creatine phosphate (already in the muscle)
Yellow
~10 sec to 2 min

Hard efforts that hurt. Lactate builds up. This is the burn you feel when you're going hard for a sustained burst.

Fuel: carbohydrate (glucose)
Green
2 min and up

Steady, sustainable effort. Long games, distance running, anything where you're going for ages.

Fuel: carbs + fat + oxygen
Key Point In MOST sports, due to the energy system requirements, carbs are the main and most important fuel for the working muscles.

    Timing — when to eat, before and during

    Eat too early and you're empty. Too late and you're sluggish. Here's the rough framework:

    Hover or tap any card to see examples and the why behind it.

    2–3 hrs before
    Real meal
    Slow-release carbs (oats, pasta, rice), some protein, easy on the fat. This is the meal that fills the tank.
    More examples

    Bircher muesli, oats with banana and honey, scrambled eggs on toast with avocado, chicken and rice, pasta with a light meat sauce.

    Why this works

    Slow-release carbs give you a steady supply of energy that's available when you need it. A bit of protein helps the meal hold you over. Easy on the fat — too much slows digestion.

    Watch out for

    Anything fried, heavy, or really high in fibre. Leave time for it to actually digest.

    1–2 hrs before
    Top-up snack
    Quicker carbs — banana, toast with honey, sports drink. Small enough to not sit heavy.
    More examples

    Oat and date cereal bar, sandwich or wrap on pita bread, banana, toast with honey or jam, small bowl of cereal with milk.

    Why this works

    Quicker carbs that digest fast so they're available as fuel by the time you start. Small portion — enough to top up, not enough to sit heavy.

    Watch out for

    Skip the protein-heavy, fat-heavy, or high-fibre stuff this close to performance.

    During (under 60 min)
    Water is fine
    For most short training sessions or games, plain water keeps you going. Sip, don't gulp.
    More examples

    Plain water, sipped regularly. Bring a labelled bottle and aim to finish it.

    Why this works

    For shorter sessions, your body has enough fuel on board. Hydration is the main job — keep small amounts going in so you don't dip.

    Watch out for

    Gulping a huge amount mid-game. Sip steadily.

    During (60+ min)
    Add carbs
    Sports drink, gel, banana, or similar. Top up little and often.
    More examples

    Sports drink, energy gel, banana, dried fruit, gummies, sports chews.

    Why this works

    Once you're past 60 minutes, your carb stores start to dip. Topping up keeps your output high right through to the end.

    Watch out for

    Trying something new on game day. Practise your during-game fuelling in training first.

    Within 30–60 min after
    Recovery snack
    Carbs + protein. Flavoured milk, sandwich, smoothie, yoghurt with fruit. Refill the tank.
    More examples

    Flavoured milk, smoothie with fruit and yoghurt, a sandwich or wrap with protein filling, pretzels, biltong, chocolate milk.

    Why this works

    The recovery window is when your body is most ready to refill carbs and rebuild muscle. Get both in, plus fluids to rehydrate.

    Watch out for

    Leaving it too long. The longer the gap, the slower the recovery.

    Evening — 2–3 hours later
    Dinner — refill and rebuild
    A proper meal to top up carbs and rebuild muscle. Vegetables matter here too — they bring the vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants your body uses to recover.
    More examples

    Lean meat, chicken, or fish. Pasta or unrefined grains. Starchy vegetables or soup. Other vegetables and salad. Olive, seed, or nut oils. Fresh fruit or fruit/dairy-based dessert.

    Why this works

    Carbs refill the energy stores you used. Protein rebuilds muscle that broke down under load. Vegetables provide micronutrients — vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants — that support recovery, immunity, and adaptation. Healthy fats help your body absorb those nutrients and regulate inflammation.

    Watch out for

    Don't skip the vegetables. Dinner is the easiest place to get them in.

    Fluids: variety helps

    A mix of fluids encourages hydration. Water, milk, smoothies, sports drinks each play a role. Sports drinks give you carbs, electrolytes, and fluid in one — useful in longer or hotter sessions.

    Carbs: your fuel

    Carbohydrates supply the fuel you need for training and competition. The harder and longer you go, the more carbs matter. Don't try to train fasted for performance — fuel the work.

    Protein: the rebuild

    Lean protein — chicken, fish, eggs, dairy, beans, tofu — helps build and repair muscle, and fills you up. Spread it across the day, not all at once.

    Vegetables: the often-missed piece

    Vegetables and fruit bring vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants. They support immunity, recovery, and long-term adaptation. Aim for variety and colour across the day.

    Fats: the regulators

    Healthy fats — olive oil, nuts, seeds, avocado — provide essential fatty acids that regulate hormones and inflammation. Easy on them close to performance, normal portions the rest of the day.

    Hydration

    Drink before you're thirsty.

    Thirst is a late warning. Once you feel it, performance has already dropped. Sip water through the day — your pee should be pale yellow. For longer games or hot days, a sports drink replaces both fluids and salts you sweat out.

    Session 03

    Recovery is where you actually get better.

    Training breaks you down. Recovery is when your body adapts and comes back stronger. Skip this part and you'll be the rat on the wheel — moving a lot, going nowhere.

    The ART of tournament performance

    Three things to focus on across a tournament day, a heavy training week, or anything that runs long.

    A
    Act
    • Stay calm under pressure — your team will follow your energy.
    • Stay focused — know why you're there, on and off the pitch.
    • Be confident, not arrogant.
    • Lead — what you do and say influences everyone around you.
    • Have fun (at the right times).
    R
    Recover
    • Plan recovery times — when do you eat, drink, rest?
    • Find time to rest. Stay out of the sun.
    • Active in between games — passive between days.
    • Sleep. Bring snacks. Hydrate fast after performing.
    • Time your food (2 hrs to break down complex meals).
    T
    Think
    • Focus on what you can control.
    • Ignore distractions — other teams, other athletes, noise.
    • Worry about you and your team alone.
    #1 Recovery Tool

    Sleep.

    It's free, you already do it, and nothing else comes close. 8–10 hours for teenagers, in a cool dark room, no screens for an hour before bed.

    Your body releases growth hormone, repairs muscle, and consolidates everything you learned in training while you sleep. Skip it and you cancel out half the work you did that day.

    Hydrate, eat, repeat.

    After performing: replace fluids straight away. Don't wait to feel thirsty.

    Snack timing: recovery food in the first 30–60 minutes. Carbs + protein together — flavoured milk, smoothie, sandwich, yoghurt with fruit.

    The next meal: a proper meal within 2 hours. Real food, normal portions.

    Recovery snacks that actually work

    Between short recovery windows (1–2 hours), pick something that's mostly carbs with a bit of protein and is easy to digest:

    — Flavoured milk
    — Fruit smoothie
    — Banana bread or fruit muffins
    — Trail mix / nut bars
    — Oat-based protein balls
    — Fresh fruit
    — Yoghurt pouches
    — Sandwich or wrap

    Active vs passive recovery

    Active recovery

    Low-intensity movement after a hard session — easy jog, swim, cycle, walk. Helps clear lactate from blood, gets blood flow to muscles.

    Use: between games / between hard reps
    vs.

    Passive recovery

    Complete rest. No exercise. Sitting, lying down, sleeping, watching a film, doing nothing.

    Use: between training days / overnight

    How do you know you're recovered?

    Three ways your body tells you. Tune in to all of them.

    Physiological

    What your body's doing
    • Resting heart rate
    • Body weight (steady)
    • Muscle soreness
    • Yesterday's performance

    Symptomatic

    What you can feel
    • Sleep quality
    • Hydration (pee colour)
    • Appetite
    • Reduced soreness

    Psychological

    What's in your head
    • Energy level
    • Mood
    • Motivation to train
    • General wellbeing
    There's no physiological difference between under-recovery and overtraining. If you keep hammering without recovering, you're a rat on a wheel — moving lots, getting nowhere.

    Teams will be split by sport. Part of the day is yours to plan: team lunch, snacks, recovery fuel — built around the energy demands of your sport, the timing of your events, and what your team will actually eat. You'll plan it. Then you'll execute it on the day.

    Your team will plan

    • Team lunch — what, when, how much for the squad
    • Between-event snacks — quick carbs, easy to digest, easy to share
    • Recovery fuel — carbs + protein within the first 30–60 min after each event
    • Hydration plan — water and/or sports drinks across the day
    • Timing — when each meal/snack happens around your events

    You'll be assessed on

    • Plan quality — does it match your sport's energy demands and the day's schedule?
